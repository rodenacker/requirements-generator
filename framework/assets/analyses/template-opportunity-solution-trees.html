How to read this tree

This is not a discovery plan — it is a structural audit. Teresa Torres's Opportunity Solution Trees are normally built top-down from a desired Outcome, through customer-interview-sourced Opportunities, to candidate Solutions and Assumption Tests. This artefact does the reverse: it reads the merged requirements/requirements.md and ladders upward from the document's features (Solutions) to the customer needs they address (Opportunities) to the single business or product goal they serve (Outcome).

What to look for. Orphan Solutions (features with no underlying customer need stated) and unaddressed Opportunities (pains the doc names but commits no Solution against) are the headline findings — they signal under-justified scope and gaps in scope respectively. Vertical-only branches (one Opportunity, one Solution, no siblings) signal premature commitment; Torres specifically warns against them. Weakly-anchored Opportunities have no keyword overlap with the Outcome's measurement clause.

Layer 4 (Assumption Tests) is sourced only from §Risks, §Assumptions, and §Open questions. Most written PRDs do not contain explicit assumptions or experiments — when those sections are absent, the layer renders a placeholder rather than fabricating tests. The absence is information, not a defect.

Provenance dots on each card show where the node was sourced from. Green = canonical source (the section type Torres or the framework's reference contract names first). Amber = derived from supplementary prose (used when the canonical source was sparse). The diagnostics block at the bottom lists every flag and gate result.
