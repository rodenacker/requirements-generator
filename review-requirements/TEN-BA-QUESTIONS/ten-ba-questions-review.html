In plain terms

This review lists the ten most pressing questions a business analyst would put back to you before this requirements document goes to design or estimation — not defects to fix, but decisions the business still needs to make. Each question carries a priority (how urgent — blocking, major, or minor), a category (which of eight business-analysis gap areas it came from), and an anchor (the §N.N section it targets, or 'missing-section' when the topic is absent from the document entirely); the ten were selected from a candidate pool (the larger set of 46 questions generated before the top ten were chosen).

One question is blocking: the activity-name-to-status mapping that drives every File Status display is never defined, so the file-log screens cannot be built faithfully until the business supplies it. The other nine are major — the unstated problem and its cost, a quantified definition of success, separation of duties, approval routing, the file source-and-format contract, failed-file and unparseable-row handling, and who consumes the export — each a default the team could guess but would carry real risk of getting wrong. Take these ten to your stakeholders and fold the answers back through /requirements before the next phase begins.

Questions

BAQ-01 — blocking · C5 Business rules & decisions · §6.2

What are the authoritative activity-name → File Status pairs behind the mapping that §6.2 BR-08 relies on and §9 flags as not enumerated?

Why this matters. File Status (§2.3, BR-08) is the spine of the file-log overview every user sees, yet §9 records that the activity-name-to-status mapping is 'not enumerated in the corpus'. Until the business supplies the activity-name → status pairs and names who maintains them, the Completed / Processing / Failed display cannot be built to reflect the real system, and any prototype mapping is a guess that may contradict production.

BAQ-02 — major · C1 Problem & justification · §1

What is the current state of transaction import and approval today, and what about it is costly or unsustainable enough to justify building this system?

Why this matters. §1 describes the solution — a dual-role import-and-approval surface — but not the problem it displaces, so there is no shared statement of what the current approach costs or why it is unsustainable. Without that anchor, scope and priority decisions float free: the team cannot tell which features relieve the real pain and which are merely nice to have.

BAQ-03 — major · C7 Edge cases & exception flows · §1.5

When a file fails processing (§2.5 Processing → Failed), what does the business want the Importer to be able to do — re-upload, request reprocessing, view the failure reason, escalate, or nothing in this release?

Why this matters. §1.5 defers the Failed-file recovery flow as 'not yet defined' while §3 records that the Importer's central fear is files failing with no clear reason — so the one path the primary user most dreads is the one the document leaves blank. The business's answer determines whether a whole recovery surface enters scope or the release ships with Failed as a dead end.

BAQ-04 — major · C2 Stakeholders & users · §6.7

Who is the downstream consumer of the exported transaction CSV (§6.7 RPT-02), and does that consumer impose any required columns, format, or naming on the export?

Why this matters. The filtered-transaction CSV (§6.7 RPT-02, F-11) leaves the system, but the document never names who consumes it or whether that consumer constrains the columns, format, or naming. If a downstream reconciliation system or auditor expects a fixed schema, the export is a contract rather than a convenience, and its column set must be confirmed before it is built.

BAQ-05 — major · C5 Business rules & decisions · §6.5

Is separation of duties a business rule — must the Approver of a transaction be a different person from the Importer who uploaded its file?

Why this matters. §1.6 states each user holds exactly one role, but the document never says whether separation of duties is a business rule — whether the person who imported a file may also approve its transactions. In a financial domain this is a core control; the answer decides whether maker-checker segregation must be reflected (or enforced) and reshapes RBAC and the approval workflow.

BAQ-06 — major · C5 Business rules & decisions · §6.1

Can any Approver action any Imported transaction, or does the business route transactions to specific approvers by file, amount, or team?

Why this matters. The document implies any Approver can action any Imported transaction (§6.1 F-08, §6.5) but never states whether transactions are routed to specific approvers by file, amount, or team. Routing to a named approver is a fundamentally different workflow — queues, assignment, hand-off — than a shared pool, and the choice changes the transaction-table and approval design.

BAQ-07 — major · C6 Data, entities & integrations · §9

What is the authoritative upstream source of the uploaded transaction files, and who owns the canonical file format the system must accept, given the format disagreements §9 records?

Why this matters. §9 records that the inputs disagree on transaction-type codes and account-number format, which signals there is no single agreed file-format contract. The business must name the authoritative upstream source and the canonical format the system must accept, or ingestion will be built against an assumption the file producers do not share.

BAQ-08 — major · C7 Edge cases & exception flows · missing-section: exception-flows

When an uploaded file contains rows that cannot be parsed or fail the expected format, what is the business policy — reject the whole file, import the valid rows and drop the invalid ones, or hold the file for correction?

Why this matters. §1.5 places file-validation-error review out of scope, but the document never states the business policy when an uploaded file contains rows that cannot be parsed. Reject-the-file, import-valid-rows-only, and hold-for-correction are materially different ingestion outcomes that change what RecordCount and File Status even mean.

BAQ-09 — major · C1 Problem & justification · missing-section: problem-justification

What does the status quo cost in measurable terms — manual rework hours, mis-keyed-transaction rates, or approval delays — that this system is expected to reduce?

Why this matters. The goals are stated qualitatively (§4.1, 'files ingested without manual rework') with no baseline for what the status quo costs in rework hours, error rates, or approval delays. Without that baseline, success cannot be measured and the business case for individual features cannot be defended at estimation time.

BAQ-10 — major · C3 Success & acceptance criteria · §4.1

What quantified target defines success for this system (for example approval turnaround time, ingestion-without-rework rate, or error reduction), and at what threshold is the business satisfied?

Why this matters. §4.1 lists quality signals as qualitative statements with no measurable threshold, so there is no agreed definition of when this system has done its job. A quantified target — approval turnaround, ingestion-without-rework rate, error reduction — is needed before the team can tell whether the build succeeded or where to concentrate effort.
